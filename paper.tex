\begin{abstract}
  This reserach aims to use the structural properties of graphs for classifications of embedded audio signals. Audio signals are embedded using a neural network
and mapped to vector space. The cosine similarity between audio samples can be used to determine how similar the samples are in harmonics, frequency, strucutre, etc.
A kNN graph is constructed, with edges connecting the k most similar audio samples, and community detection finds patterns of similar groupings. By using a grpah, a
more nuanced view of audio classification is revealed by showing how audio does not often belong to just one category, but has similarties to many differnt types of audio.
The results of this paper show that community detection can be and effective way to determine classification groups and create the basis of a GNN, where a lower k-value will
create more discerning clusters, and a higher k-value will show more general clustering.
\end{abstract}


\section{Introduction}
Classifying audio signals is a complex problem which continues to be the subject of research. Most recently, audio classification has been performed with the help of neural networks. This research project aims to use kNN graphs of embedded audio to improve sound classification by encoding similarities between sounds. 

\subsection{Context and Background}
Audio classification and genre detection are an ongoing area of reserach. Machine learning has shown great benefits to improve audio classification. One method is through embeddings. Built off of the principles in word2vec\cite{mikolov}, audio embedding transforms audio into vectors. The angle between audio signals represents their similartiy.
This similarity can be quantified with the cosine of the angle between the vectors. A graph can be constructed from these embeddings, where each node represents an audio sample, and edges encode the cosine similarities between them. To perform classification, groups of similar audio signals must be determined. This is achieved with
Leiden clustering\cite{traag}. Communities are a group of nodes which are more densely connected to each other than nodes outside. Leiden clustering aims to create well-defined and meaningful communities by maximizing modularity. The algorithm is composed of three main phases:
\begin{enumerate}[noitemsep]
  \item Local Moving of Nodes: Nodes are intially assigned to individual clusters, then moved between clusters to maximize modularity.
  \item Refinement of Partitions: The internal structure of each community is refined to ensure no disconnected nodes.
  \item Aggregation into Super-nodes: The network's strucutre is simplfied by aggregating the nodes in each community into one super-node.
\end{enumerate}
For this paper, the third step is ignored. Analyzing the quality of the communities is an important piece of this research, which is not easily accomplished through a super-node.

\subsection{Problem Statement and Research Question}
This paper aims to improve audio classification by using the properities of graphs to encode audio structure. This research will answer the question: "Can audio embedding graphs improve sound classification?"

\subsection{Significance of the Study}
This paper is impactful in that it uses graphs and audio embedding for audio classification. This work could have future applications in genre detection, identifying similar songs, or identifying audio as speech, music, or environmental sound.

\section{Literature Review}
  The field of audio classification has been researched for many years. Most recently, the field has made significant strides by leveraging machine learning techniques. The benefit of using graph properties to improve classification, either by representing the audio as a graph or through classification on a graph of multiple signals, has been well explored.
  
  The paper “Content Analysis for Audio Classification and Segmentation”\cite{lu} by Lie Lu, 
Hong-Jiang Zhang and Hao Jiang in 2002 presents a method for audio classification as speech, 
music, environmental sound, or silence. It was published by the peer-reviewed magazine IEEE 
Transactions on Speech and Audio Processing. This paper aims to classify the type of audio 
and segment speech audio based on the speaker. The paper uses the K-Nearest Neighbors (KNN) 
algorithm applied to the zero-crossing rate of a frame to differentiate between different 
types of signals. Speech signals often display a pattern of alternating voiced and unvoiced 
sounds that music signals do not, which can be identified by the KNN. Silence can be determined 
by an average short-time energy and a zero-crossing rate lower than a given threshold. 
Discriminating music and environmental sounds can be achieved with band periodicity, spectrum 
flux, and noise frame ratio. Segmentation is achieved using Linear Spectral Pairs to 
discriminate between two speakers. The final results found that this method worked well for 
discriminating between music, speech, and environmental noises. Speaker discrimination worked 
fairly well, but struggled to detect the same speaker in different environments. 

  Neural networks have proven useful for audio classification. The paper "Graph-Based Audio Classification Using
Pre-Trained Models and Graph Neural Networks"\cite{castro-ospina} by A. E. Castro-Ospina, M. A. Solarte-Sanchez,
L. S. Vega-Escobar, C. Isaza, and J. D. Martinez-Vargas explores a graph-based neural network for this task. The proposed methodology uses pre-trained models
designed to extract deep features from audio files, which are used as the nodes of the graph. Two audio datasets are used, which are mapped to feature space using the three embedding strategies, VGGish, PANN, and YAMNet. A kNN graph is constructed by creating an edge from each embedding to its kNN nearest neighbors. Three GNNs were used on the graph to classify the audio: GCN, GraphSAGE, and GAT. The results showed that graphs are a very useful way to extract audio information and that GNNs obtain competitive performance in audio classification.

The paper “Genre 
Classification Empowered by Knowledge-Embedded Music Representation” \cite{ding} by H. Ding, L. Zhai, C. 
Zhao, F. Wang, G. Wang, and W. Xi, published by IEEE in 2024, researches music genre classification specifically. Using two different music datasets, a knowledge graph is built that embeds relationships between genres, artists, and instruments. The knowledge graph is novel for its use of two branches, the audio branch for extracting features from the original audio, and the KG branch to extract knowledge-aware features. Using a model inspired by Node2Vec, the knowledge graph is used to classify an audio signal based on where it falls in the feature space. The results showed significant superiority over existing methods.

Another approach is to represent audio as a graph and perform classification on the audio graph embeddings.  C. Aironi, S. Cornell, E. Principi, and S. Squartini in the paper "This was researched by Graph-based Representation of Audio signals for Sound Event Classification"\cite{Aironi}. In their approach, the graph is derived from the log-Mel scale spectrogram. Two classification methods are performed, one with a GNN and one with a CNN+GNN combination. Although the model performed worse than a state-of-the-art CNN for audio classification, the results showed that the graph embedding was able to capture important underlying features that were otherwise missed.

An additional area of research is zero-learning, in which a model must classify data from categories that were not present in the testing data by using auxiliary features. 

\section{Methodology}

\subsection{Research Design}
This research is an experimental study to classify sound through graph community detection. The nodes of the graph will be composed of i audio samples, ai. Audio samples are sourced from FreeSound and Free Music Archive, which house open-source audio samples available for download. For this research, audio, speech, and environmental sounds were used. Each audio sample is represented by a feature vector zi, which comprises the node features. Feature vectors are learned through audio embedding via Contrastive Language-Audio Pretraining (CLAP), a pre-trained neural network for audio to vector embedding. Using a pretrained neural network was determined to be the best choice for this project, because building and developing a new neural network is not the focus of the research and is outside the scope of the project. Simpler methods, which do not require a neural network, were considered, such as the log-mel spectrogram averaged over time, but this would not be nearly as robust. An audio embedding from a neural network gives the best representation of the audio sample by capturing timbre, texture, semantic similarity, and some temporal structure. It is also less sensitive to noise, time shifts, and small spectral variations. CLAP was chosen over other models because it was trained on large audio datasets containing multiple different kinds of audio, including the three used in this project (speech, environmental sounds, and music). It is a good choice for similarity detection because it also learns and embeds semantic similarity.
  A kNN graph is built by connecting each audio sample to its top k nearest neighbors, evaluated on cosine similarity. Cosine similarity measures the angle between vectors, which represents similarity between audio embeddings. Magnitude is often arbitrary and noisy; thus is not a necessary measurement. For this project, which focuses on small graphs, the values of k will be 5 and 10. Edges will only be added if they have a similarity score in the top 70\% of calculated scores. This limits the edges of audio embeddings that are dissimilar from the rest of the dataset. 

  After the graph is built, the Leiden Algorithm is used for community detection to determine audio clusters from the graph. Each audio signal is labeled prior to the embedding with the correct classification. After the graph is built and communities are detected, the silhouette coefficient and adjusted random index are calculated as measures of the strength of the clusterings.

  Two datasets are used in this research. The first contains a mix of 29 audio signals, broken down as follows: 10 speech, 7 singing (without music), 11 instrumental music, and one constant tone. The second set contains music of five different genres: pop, country, rock, hip-hop, and R\&B. Five songs for each genre are sampled. Community detection with a k value of 5 and 10 was performed for each dataset. For this research, labels are not assigned to the clusters, as this would require further processing by a neural network. The goal of this project is to group together similar sounds. For this reason, intrinsic metrics are used to evaluate the quality of the clusters, which do not require a ground truth. Three metrics are evaluated in this paper:

\begin{enumerate}[noitemsep]
  \item Modularity: Measures the strength of division of the network by measuring the weight of edges within the cluster versus out of the cluster. The Leiden algorithm clusters nodes by maximizing modularity. Values above 0.3 often indicate more internal edges than random chance.
  \item Silhouette Coefficient\cite{rousseeuw}: Measures how well a point fits in its cluster when compared to other clusters. It is calculated with the mean intra-cluster distance and the mean nearest-cluster distance for each sample. Values range from -1 to 1, with higher scores indicating better clustering.
  \item Daives-Bouldin Score\cite{davies}: Measures the average similarity of each cluster with its most similar cluster, where similarity is measured as the ratio of within-cluster distances to between-cluster distances. The DB score gives insight into the between-cluster separation. Lower scores indicate separation.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Key Observations}
Community detection is able to reflect the cosine similarities discovered by the heatmap, but also reveals patterns that are not at first obvious. Graphs which have the most clear clustering include audio signals
where the groups are very clearly different from each other. A higher k value will result in a more general differentiation (i.e., speech versus music), while a lower k value will reveal more nuanced details.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Interpretation of Results}
This research reveals that graphs can be an effective tool for genre classification. The patterns observed in the heatmap became more apparent and amplified as the audio was graphed. The graph provided a "sliding scale" of audio types, shifting from one type to another throughout the graph, with the most dissimilar audio types on opposite ends.

The mixed audio dataset grouped speech and music on oppoite ends. This is expected, as speech and music often take on very different spectral representations. Singing was placed in between, leaning closer to speech, but with the rhytmic elements of music samples. These patterns are discovered by the audio embedding, and revealed in the graph structure.
A low k value was more discerning, by creating more communities, while a higher k value revealed more general patterns.

The community detection on the music dataset grouped similar music genres together. R\&B and hip-hop were grouped together at one end of the graph. This result is not surprising, because hip-hop was born out of rapping over R\&B beats. They often have very similar production styles and beats. Towards the middle of the graph, rock and country were grouped
together. Again, these are two music genres that are very similar, and contain many similar elements. The other far extreme of the graph contains two heavy, electronic pop songs, which sound very dissimilar to the red of the dataset. These songs have fewer edges than the rest of the dataset, because they were the edges trimmed by the threshold. In between
this community and the rock/country community, there is a "transition" community of mainly pop and rock. The heavier pop songs are connected to this community through heavy rock songs. This graph provides an interesting view of genre as not a binary classification, but instead a specturm. Although songs may be different genres, such as R\&B and hip-hop,
the graph structure reveals similarities between them that are backed up by the historical factors of these genres. 

\subsection{Implications}
These results have implications in the field of audio classification and music detection. By leveraging graph properties, a more detailed picture of an audio
sample can be painted. Audio classification is often not a binary decision, as audio signals are often composed of multiple different parts (ie, singing and instrumental music, and music sample which takes inspiration of multiple genres, etc.)
Graphing these signals can reveal patterns and connections which may not be obvious by simply looking a single value.

\subsection{Limitations}
This study was greatly limited by a small sample size. It is difficult to draw conclusions from two small datasets. One other limitation of this study was the community detection algorithm selected. The Leiden algorithm works by maximizing modularity,
which is computed based on the ratio of edges "in" the community verses edges "out" of the community. Because each node is forced to have k edges, this means edges may be added to somewhat dissimilar audio signals, especially for audio signal that are
unique, or when the k-value exceeds the number of samples in a given community. This is handled sightly by the threshold, which removes the bottom 30\% of edges, but it may be more effective to determine a constant value that the cosine similarity must reach before an edge is added between two nodes. This may remove more
"out" edges, and may be an effective way to separate audio which is similar but should still be in different communities (ie, speech and singing)

\subsection{Suggestions for Future Research}
The first step to expand this research is collect more audio samples. Additionally, it would be beenficial to dive deeper into the exact audio signals and why they were grouped where.
For example, examining the signal structure of an incorrectly classified sound, or listening to a music sample on the edge of two communities to determine if it might fit into both.
It would also be worth comparing to another metric, such as the Mel-frequency cepstral coefficient, used to model human hearing, to numerically determine if audio that is grouped together
sounds similar to the human ear.

Finally, determining a song's genre is often a difficult task, open to discretion and interpretation. Many songs fit into more than one category. This research grouped songs into only one
genre, based on what is considered the dominating genre. However, labeling songs with a more nuanced view of genre (ie, including mutliple genres on one song) could provide extra explanations
for the edges added and the communities detected.

\section{Conclusion}

  The research took advantage of graph structure to expand audio classification with audio embeddings. The results of this study show that graphs
can reveal patterns between audio that are not immediately obvious, but expand upon the idea of audio classification as a binary task. Graph
clustering algorithms were shown to be an effective way to find groups of similar audio signals.

  This research adds to the field of audio classification by exploring a new method of finding similar audio samples. Audio and genre classification is
important and rapidly evolving. This research shows that taking advantage of machine learning and graph theory can expand audio classification from a binary
to a sliding scale.

  Although this research was limited in its scope and size, expanding upon it could reveal interesting results and new methods of audio classification.
These methods could be used as a base for a Graph Neural Network to find songs that sound similar, strip vocals from music, determine the genres of a song, or group audio samples into similar groups.
