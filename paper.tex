\section{Style}
Write clearly and simply in the third person for an audience that is well-grounded in computing, but who may have limited exposure or knowledge about the specific topic of your paper. Define any technical terms deemed to require clarification when they are introduced.

\section{Introduction}
Use sections and subsections to guide the reader. If the section title shown contains an "or", you may choose to use either name (do not use both). You may adapt the subsections as needed.

\subsection{Context and Background}
Present general information on the topic. Highlight the broader context and relevance of the research.

\subsection{Problem Statement and Research Question}
Clearly state the gap in knowledge or the problem your study addresses. Articulate the main research question(s) or hypothesis/hypotheses.

\subsection{Significance of the Study}
Explain why your research is necessary or valuable. Discuss theoretical, practical, or policy implications.

\subsection{Scope and Organization}
Briefly outline what the reader can expect in the upcoming sections.

\subsection{Code Listings}
Short, relevant code snippets or pseudocode can be included inline if they are important for enhancing understanding, provided they are well-formatted and clearly labeled. Commands from \texttt{listings} package allow you to display code easily with customizable coloring and styling rules. Here is an example.

\begin{lstlisting}[language=Python,  caption=Python example]
x = 42
epsilon = 0.01
step = epsilon**2
num_guesses = 0
ans = 0.0
while abs(ans**2-x) > epsilon and ans < x:
    ans = ans + step
    num_guesses += 1
if abs(ans**2-x) <= epsilon:
    print(str(ans) +
    ' is close to the square root of ' +
    str(x))
else:
    print('Failed to find square root of ' + str(x))
print("The number of guesses is " + str(num_guesses))
\end{lstlisting}

\subsection{Figures}

The ``\verb|figure|'' environment should be used for figures. One or
more images can be placed within a figure. If your figure contains
third-party material, you must clearly identify it as such, as shown
in the example Figure \ref{fig:theRR} below.
\begin{figure}[h]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{theRRimage}
  \caption{A screenshot of the music video for "Never gonna give you up", performed by Rick Astley. Screenshot via Wikipedia. (\protect\url{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rickrolling}).}
  \label{fig:theRR}
\end{figure}

Your figures should contain a caption which describes the figure to
the reader. Figure captions are placed {\itshape below} the figure.

Every figure should also have a figure description unless it is purely
decorative. These descriptions convey what’s in the image to someone
who cannot see it. They are also used by search engine crawlers for
indexing images, and when images cannot be loaded.

A figure description must be unformatted plain text less than 2000
characters long (including spaces).  {\bfseries Figure descriptions
  should not repeat the figure caption – their purpose is to capture
  important information that is not already provided in the caption or
  the main text of the paper.} For figures that convey important and
complex new information, a short text description may not be
adequate. More complex alternative descriptions can be placed in an
appendix and referenced in a short figure description. For example,
provide a data table capturing the information in a bar chart, or a
structured list representing a graph.  For additional information
regarding how best to write figure descriptions and why doing this is
so important, please see
\url{https://www.acm.org/publications/taps/describing-figures/}.

\subsection{Key Observations}
Summarize significant findings without interpreting them in depth—save interpretation for the Discussion section (unless Results and Discussion are combined).

\subsection{Statistical or Thematic Findings}
Report p-values, effect sizes, confidence intervals, or themes, depending on methodology.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Interpretation of Results}
Connect your findings to your original research questions. Explain how the results support or challenge existing literature/theories.

\subsection{Implications}
Discuss practical, theoretical, or policy implications. Show how your work advances knowledge in the field.

\subsection{Limitations}
Acknowledge constraints such as sample size, method-specific limitations, or biases. Suggest how these limitations affect interpretation and generalizability.

\subsection{Suggestions for Future Research}
Propose follow-up studies or alternative methods that address unanswered questions.
